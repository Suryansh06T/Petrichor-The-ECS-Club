Effect of CO2 on Temperature

Global Warming Model
AIM

Demonstrate how increased CO2 levels trap heat energy.

OBSERVATION

Two bottles—one with air, one with CO2—are placed under a light source. The temperature rise is recorded to show the greenhouse effect in action.

The CO2 bottle consistently retains more heat, mirroring atmospheric warming.

Volunteers: 2

Groundwater Depletion & Recharge

A Visual Aquifer Model

This working model uses layers of sand, gravel, and clay to represent an aquifer. It visually demonstrates:

  • Depletion: Rapid water level drop due to excessive pumping.
  • Recharge: How rainwater harvesting slowly restores the water table.
KEY LESSON

Natural recharge is often slower than human extraction.

Volunteers: 2

Temperature Inversion

Why Pollution Gets Trapped
CONCEPT

Demonstrating how cold air traps smoke near the ground.

Using a match and a compressed bottle, this model simulates winter smog conditions.

When pressure changes, the smoke creates a dense cloud that cannot escape, visually explaining why air quality drops significantly during winter inversions.

Volunteers: 2

High in the Sky

The Venturi Effect
ISSUE

High-rise buildings causing beach erosion.

Tall buildings can accelerate wind speed by forcing air through narrow gaps (the Venturi Effect).

This simulation projects wind flow patterns to hypothesize how new structures might unintentionally strip sand from beaches and deposit it elsewhere.

Volunteers: 2

Deep Depression

Sand Dune Erosion Control
PROBLEM

Wind blowouts in sand dunes.

A "blowout" occurs when wind funnels through a gap in a dune, eroding it rapidly.

This experiment uses a sandbox and fan to test methods for stopping this erosion, such as planting model vegetation or building windbreaks to stop the "depression" from growing.

Volunteers: 2

Seismograph Experiment

Detecting Earthquakes
CHALLENGE

Can we predict the direction of an impact?

By constructing a lateral motion seismograph using a suspended weight and marbles, this model attempts to trace the direction of a shockwave.

It demonstrates the principles of inertia and wave propagation used in real seismic stations.

Volunteers: 2

The Classic Eruption

Volcanic Eruption Experiment
PRINCIPLE

Chemical reaction releasing carbon dioxide gas.

A simple but effective model of volcanic activity. By mixing baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) with an acid like vinegar (acetic acid), a rapid chemical reaction occurs.

This releases carbon dioxide gas, which creates a bubbling, erupting lava flow.

Volunteers: 2

Cloud in a Bottle

Cloud Formation Experiment
PRINCIPLE

Pressure change and condensation.

A demonstration of how clouds form. By pressurizing a bottle containing isopropyl alcohol vapor and then rapidly releasing the pressure, the sudden cooling causes the vapor to condense into visible cloud droplets.

Volunteers: 2

Erosion in Action

Canyon Formation Experiment
PRINCIPLE

Water erosion and sediment transport.

By flowing water through a sand-filled tray, we simulate how rivers carve through rock over time.

The moving water picks up and transports sediment, gradually creating a deep channel.

Volunteers: 2

Soil Erosion in a Bottle

Erosion Experiment
PRINCIPLE

Vegetation's role in preventing soil loss.

A comparative experiment to demonstrate how plants protect soil.

Runoff from bare soil is dark and sediment-heavy, while vegetated soil's runoff is cleaner, illustrating the importance of root systems.

Volunteers: 2

Clean Water from Earth

Natural Filtration Experiment
PRINCIPLE

Filtration and absorption by soil layers.

By layering different types of earth materials like gravel, sand, and charcoal in a container, we can create a simple filter.

Dirty water is poured through, and as it passes through each layer, impurities are trapped and absorbed, resulting in cleaner water.

Volunteers: 2

Geostationary Satellite Model

Orbital Mechanics Experiment
PRINCIPLE

Matching Earth's rotation for fixed-point observation.

By using a rotating globe and a model satellite on a fixed arm, we demonstrate how an object at a specific altitude and velocity can revolve at the same rate as the Earth.

This makes the satellite appear stationary over a single point.

Volunteers: 2

Can You Feel The Difference?

Texture & Geology
GOAL

Separate granite, shale, and sandstone by texture alone using touch.

  • Sandstone: Layering.
  • Shale: Pressure formed.
  • Granite: Melting/Cooling.

This experiment tests if distinct geological processes result in distinct, palpable textures.

Volunteers: 2

Plated Guesses

Reconstructing Pangaea
THEORY

Plate Tectonics & Continental Drift.

Scientists theorize that all continents were once a supercontinent called Pangaea.

In this activity, we trace and cut out the continents to see if they fit together like a jigsaw puzzle, demonstrating geological drift.

Volunteers: 2

What You See Is Not What You Get

Mineral Streak Test
PRINCIPLE

Identification by powder color.

A mineral's surface color can be misleading due to oxidation or impurities.

The "streak test" involves rubbing the mineral on an unglazed porcelain tile to reveal its true powder color. This is a reliable diagnostic tool for geologists.
